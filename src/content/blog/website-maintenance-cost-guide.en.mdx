"We handle the website ourselves. It doesn't really cost us anything."

This is one of the most common things small business owners tell us — chiropractors, florists, tatami shops, construction firms. And on the surface, it makes sense. Pay for hosting and a domain, and your site keeps running. So what's the problem?

The problem is that **free-looking maintenance usually has hidden costs**. This guide breaks down three common approaches honestly — including when each one actually makes sense.

## The hidden costs of DIY maintenance

A DIY setup looks like ¥0/month on paper. In practice, it has three real costs that rarely get counted.

**Your time.** Updating WordPress plugins, swapping out images, checking that your contact form still works — individually these feel like nothing. Together, they add up to several hours a month. If those hours are coming from your actual work, they're not free.

**The slip risk.** When you're busy, updates get deferred. When plugins go unpatched, your site becomes a target. The majority of WordPress compromises happen because of outdated plugins — not because hackers specifically targeted you, but because automated scanners found an unpatched version. Many business owners only discover this when a customer calls to say the site looks strange.

**The nobody's-watching problem.** If your contact form breaks, nobody will tell you. If your site slows to a crawl, visitors will just leave. The silence isn't safety — it's a blind spot.

## Reactive vs. proactive: the key difference

Most web agency maintenance plans are **reactive** — they respond to problems you report. That's useful, but it means someone has to notice the problem first. Usually that someone is a frustrated customer.

WebMori's approach is **proactive** — every month, an engineer runs automated scans and manual checks across security and performance, then sends you a plain-language PDF report. You find out about issues before your customers do.

This isn't a criticism of agencies. It's a different model, suited to different needs.

## What good monthly maintenance should include

Whether you're doing it yourself or paying someone else, these five checks should happen every month:

- **Security scan:** Are your CMS, plugins, or themes carrying known vulnerabilities?
- **Software updates:** Is everything on the latest stable version?
- **Performance check:** Is your mobile load-time score holding up?
- **Backup confirmation:** Did the most recent backup complete successfully?
- **Form test:** Are contact and booking forms actually delivering messages?

This baseline alone prevents the majority of serious problems. If any of these haven't been checked recently, your site may already have issues you don't know about.

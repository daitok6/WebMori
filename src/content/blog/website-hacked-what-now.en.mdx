"We're too small to be a target" — that thinking is exactly what makes small businesses vulnerable.

According to Japan's IPA (Information-technology Promotion Agency), **roughly 60% of cyberattack victims are companies with fewer than 100 employees**. Large companies invest heavily in security, which makes smaller, less-protected sites far easier targets. Attackers don't cherry-pick — they scan at scale and hit whatever's open.

So what actually happens if your site gets hit? And what does it cost?

## What happens after a hack

### You lose customer trust

If customer data from your contact forms or e-commerce store gets exposed, the question "Can I trust this company with my personal information?" is impossible to unask. Rebuilding trust takes far longer than you'd expect — and under Japan's Personal Information Protection Act, a data breach triggers a **legal obligation to notify affected customers**.

### Revenue stops

When your site gets defaced or infected with malware, you may need to take it offline entirely. For e-commerce sites, **even one day of downtime can mean tens of thousands of yen in lost sales**.

### You disappear from Google

Google flags malware-infected sites with a "This site may be harmful" warning. Traffic drops immediately, and it can take **weeks for the warning to be removed** — and even longer for your search rankings to recover to where they were.

### Recovery is expensive

Depending on the severity, hiring a specialist to restore your site can cost **¥300,000 to over ¥1,000,000**. If customer data was exposed, add legal notification costs, potential regulatory penalties, and the time cost on top.

## What the timeline looks like

The damage compounds the longer it goes undetected — and most victims don't notice immediately.

**Day 1:** Attacker compromises your site. You don't know yet.

**Days 2–3:** Google detects the malware. A "dangerous site" warning starts appearing for visitors.

**1 week:** Search rankings drop significantly. Inquiries and sales start falling.

**1 month:** You hire a specialist. Recovery work begins. Costs become clear.

**2–3 months:** Google warning is removed. SEO rankings begin recovering.

**The problem is that "acting when you notice" is already too late.** By the time most site owners realize something is wrong, 1–2 weeks have already passed.

## How attacks typically happen

**Outdated software** — WordPress plugins, themes, and platform updates include security patches. Not updating means known vulnerabilities stay open. This is the most common attack vector by far.

**Weak passwords** — A short or reused admin password can be cracked in minutes by automated tools.

**Vulnerable forms** — Poorly built contact or login forms can expose your database to attackers.

## 3 things you can do today

### 1. Keep everything updated

WordPress, Shopify apps, themes, plugins — update everything to the latest version. Enable auto-updates where possible.

### 2. Use strong passwords

Your admin password should be at least 12 characters with a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) if available.

### 3. Check your site regularly

Once a month, look for anything unusual. If you're not sure what to look for, [this article](/en/blog/five-signs-security-checkup) walks through 5 specific signs to check.

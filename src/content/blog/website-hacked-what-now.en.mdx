## "We're too small to be a target" — think again

Many small business owners assume hackers only go after big corporations. The reality is the opposite: smaller websites are often easier targets.

The reason is simple. Large companies invest heavily in security, while smaller sites often have fewer protections — making them low-hanging fruit for attackers.

According to Japan's IPA (Information-technology Promotion Agency), **roughly 60% of cyberattack victims are companies with fewer than 100 employees**.

## What actually happens after a hack

### You lose customer trust

If customer data from your contact forms or e-commerce store gets leaked, people start wondering whether it's safe to do business with you. Rebuilding that trust takes far longer than you'd expect.

### Revenue stops

When your site gets defaced or infected with malware, you may have to take it offline entirely. For an e-commerce site, **even one day of downtime can mean tens of thousands of yen in lost sales**.

### You disappear from Google

Google flags sites with detected malware, showing a "This site may be harmful" warning to visitors. Traffic drops immediately, and it can take weeks for the warning to be removed — and even longer for your search rankings to recover.

### Recovery is expensive

Depending on the severity, hiring a specialist to clean and restore your site can cost **300,000 to over 1,000,000 yen**. If customer data was exposed, add legal notification costs and potential liability on top of that.

## Common ways attacks happen

Without getting too technical, here are the most common "entry points" for attacks:

**Outdated software** — Not updating your WordPress plugins, themes, or platform means known vulnerabilities stay open. This is the single most common attack vector.

**Weak passwords** — If your admin password is "password123" or your company name, automated tools can crack it in minutes.

**Vulnerable forms** — Poorly built contact or login forms can let attackers extract data from your database.

## 3 things you can do today

### 1. Keep everything updated

WordPress, Shopify apps, themes, plugins — update them all to the latest version. Enable auto-updates where possible.

### 2. Use strong passwords

Your admin password should be at least 12 characters with a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols. Set up two-factor authentication (2FA) if available.

### 3. Check your site regularly

Even a monthly check for anything unusual goes a long way. If you're not sure what to look for, an audit service like WebMori can do it for you.

Waiting until after a hack to take action is too late. Small steps today can protect your business from significant damage tomorrow.

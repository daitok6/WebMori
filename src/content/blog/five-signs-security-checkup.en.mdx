## When was your last checkup?

Cars have inspections. People have annual physicals. But surprisingly few business owners ever give their website a regular checkup.

A website isn't something you build once and forget about. The longer it sits without attention, the more risks quietly pile up. If any of the following 5 signs sound familiar, it might be time for a closer look.

## Sign 1: Nothing's been updated in 6+ months

WordPress plugins, themes, Shopify apps — when was the last time you hit "update"?

Software updates regularly include security patches. If you haven't updated in over 6 months, **known vulnerabilities may be sitting wide open** on your site.

Think of it like leaving your front door with a broken lock for half a year.

## Sign 2: Your password is "the usual one"

Is your admin password the same one you use for other services?

If any of those services gets breached, attackers will try that same email-password combination on other sites. This is called a "credential stuffing" attack.

**Your admin password should be unique — not shared with anything else.**

## Sign 3: Browsers show "Not Secure" warnings

Does your browser show a "Not Secure" label or missing padlock icon in the address bar?

This means your SSL certificate (HTTPS) isn't properly configured. To visitors, it looks like a warning that your site might be dangerous — and bounce rates go up significantly.

It also hurts your Google search rankings.

## Sign 4: Unknown pages or links appearing on your site

Have you noticed pages or links on your site that you didn't create?

When a site gets compromised, attackers often inject spam pages or phishing links that are hard to spot. Try searching "site:yourdomain.com" on Google to see all indexed pages.

If anything looks unfamiliar, you need to act immediately.

## Sign 5: Your contact form is flooded with spam

Are you getting dozens of junk submissions through your forms every day?

While it seems like just a nuisance, mass spam through your forms is **a sign that your form's security is weak**. Attackers can exploit form vulnerabilities as a stepping stone for more serious attacks.

Adding reCAPTCHA or reviewing your form configuration can help.

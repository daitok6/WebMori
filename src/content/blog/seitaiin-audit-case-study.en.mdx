"Our website looks fine. We take LINE bookings. We have good reviews. I'm sure we're okay."

That was the baseline assumption when a Tokyo-area acupuncture and chiropractic clinic reached out for a WebMori audit. The prompt wasn't panic — it was a nudge. A similar clinic nearby had their site compromised, and the owner decided a quick look couldn't hurt.

The audit came back with **9 issues**.

## Who the client was

A well-established clinic operating for over a decade in Tokyo. Built on WordPress. Integrated with LINE for appointment bookings — the kind of setup that's common across independent healthcare practices in Japan. Over 30 Google reviews, a clean mobile-friendly design, and a steady stream of patients.

In other words: not a neglected, cobbled-together website. A real business site that looked professionally done. "We thought there were no particular problems" wasn't wishful thinking — it was a reasonable read of what was visible from the surface.

But security problems don't live on the surface.

## What the audit found

### Critical: Appointment notifications could be forged

The most serious issue: **LINE webhook signature verification was not implemented**.

Here's what that means in plain terms. When a patient books through LINE, the LINE platform sends a notification to the clinic's website — something like "New appointment from [patient name]." The website is supposed to verify that this message actually came from LINE, not from someone pretending to be LINE. That verification is called signature checking.

Without it, **anyone with basic technical knowledge could send fake appointment notifications** to the system. Fabricated bookings. Overwritten appointment data. Patient information manipulated without anyone noticing — because the site had no way to tell a real LINE message from a fake one.

This wasn't a theoretical risk. It was a live gap with no mechanism stopping it.

### High: Patient data accessible without logging in

The site had an API — a behind-the-scenes channel used to manage bookings and contact submissions. It should only be accessible to logged-in administrators. **It wasn't protected by any authentication.**

Anyone who knew (or could guess) the right URL could pull up booking records and contact form submissions from outside. No username, no password required. From a privacy standpoint, this was a serious problem that needed fixing immediately.

### Medium: Four issues in the middle tier

- **Outdated WordPress plugins**: Three plugins hadn't been updated in over 12 months, each carrying known security vulnerabilities that patches had already addressed
- **No rate limiting on the contact form**: The form accepted unlimited submissions with no throttling — a wide-open channel for spam and automated abuse
- **Images not optimized**: Full-resolution photos loading on the homepage, adding several seconds to load time on mobile — which is how most patients would visit the site
- **Admin session timeout too long**: Once logged in, the session stayed active for an unusually extended period, creating a risk if a device was ever lost or left unattended

### Low: Two minor issues

- **Missing meta descriptions**: Search result snippets were being auto-generated rather than written, which likely cost clicks from potential patients searching for a local clinic
- **Hardcoded Japanese strings in the codebase**: Not a security issue, but something that would create extra work in any future site update or expansion
